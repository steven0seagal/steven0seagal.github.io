Title of Thesis Advanced methods of analyzing genomic neighborhoods to provide function prediction for new, uncharacterized gene families.

Years 2018-2024

University Institute of Biochemistry and Biophysics, Polish Academy of Sciences


Show/Hide Abstract

Abstract: Biochemical processes form a complex network of dependencies, therefore, a deep analysis of the individual components of genomes, especially genes, is currently essential. Determining the function of a gene is a key task in genomics applicable e.g. in evolutionary biology, drug discovery and improvement, and personalized medicine. The main objective of this doctoral thesis was to develop a solution enabling the analysis of the genomic neighborhood of any bacterial gene to determine the biological function of an unknown gene. The work began with the creation of an algorithm enabling the analysis of genomic neighborhoods. A widely accessible tool was developed - a server and an extensive database containing bacterial genomes, which allows for performing analyses. This doctoral dissertation describes three projects. In each of them, analyses of genomic neighborhoods were utilized, which were performed using the method developed within the dissertation, as well as other bioinformatics methods. In the scope of the first project, the diversity of bacterial families of Protein Kinase-Like (PKL) proteins was studied. As a result of the conducted analyses, protein domains were identified, which significantly often appeared in the analyzed neighborhoods. These essential protein domains were subject to further analysis in terms of their presence in all PKL proteins, aiming to understand their potential common functions. As a result of the conducted analyses, common domains in neighborhoods for certain groups of PKL protein families were identified. However, for individual PKL protein families, the presence of domains in neighborhoods was observed, which can be considered characteristic for them and well conserved in the evolution process of bacterial genomes. In the second project, a characterization of genomic neighborhoods was conducted on a larger scale, utilizing genomes of bacteria belonging to the Legionella genus. The main aim of these projects however, was to prepare a basis for the creation of a more comprehensive and complex solution, which was presented in the third project. The proposed tool - ProFaNA (Protein Family Neighborhood Analysis) - uses experiences from previous projects and enables analyses on a much larger scale. ProFaNA allows for the analysis of genomic neighborhoods based on an extensive database containing nearly 90 thousand bacterial genomes, as well as enabling analysis within defined taxonomic groups. ProFaNA allows for the examination of repeatable patterns of gene neighborhoods and identification of protein families potentially functionally linked, which constitutes a significant step in expanding our knowledge about the functions of bacterial genes.

Title of Thesis Detection and characterization of extracellular hydrolases from bacteria of the genus Stenotrophomonas

Years 2015-2017

University Cathedra of Biochemistry, Warsaw University of Life Sciences

Abstract: Chitinases are group of enzymes belongs to glycoside hydrolases which break the β-1,4 glycoside bond in chitin molecule. Chitynolytic properties can be used in medicine, waste management or biocontrol. In this study we optimize growth condition of Stenotrophomonas rhizophila. Moreover during experiments we had detected, determined and partially characterize chitinolytic activity of extracellular endochitinase syntetise from Stenotrophomonas rhizophila. Also there was a try to purify tested protein and we had obtained part of nucleotide sequence that codes enzyme.

Title of Thesis Devil facial tumour disease is causing next reduction of population size or near extinction of Tasmanian devil (Sarcophilus harrisii)?

Years 2012-2015

University Department of Genetics and General Animal Breeding , Warsaw University of Life Sciences


Show/Hide Abstract

Abstract: Devil facial tumour disease is causing next reduction in the population size or near extinction of Tasmanian devil (Sarcophilus harrisii)? Tasmanian devil is the largest carnivorous marsupial on Tasmania Island since at the beginning of XX century thylacines had extinct. Genetic diversity observed in all devils divided this species in two populations, northwest and east. Tasmanian devils are legally protected for over 70 years and since 1996 devils are threatened by extinction caused by fast developing disease that comes from a Schwann cell, Devil Facial Tumor Disease (DFTD). This disease is malignancy and transferred from animal to animal by tumor’s cells. Nowadays there is not known cure for the DFTD, however there are some research that main goal is to create a vaccine against this disease. This thesis shows three aspects; characteristic of Tasmanian devil’s species and connection between behavior and transfer of Devil Facial Tumor (DFT), genetic diversity in free-living devils and validity of division into east and northwest population and accurate presentation of Devil Facial Tumor Disease.
